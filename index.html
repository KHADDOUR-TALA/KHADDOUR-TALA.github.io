About Me

My journey into robotics began not with circuits and code, but with observing my grandfather's struggle with mobility after a stroke. Witnessing the gap between human need and technological capability ignited my passion for creating intelligent systems that restore human potential.

As an undergraduate at Manara University, I specialized in intelligent control systems, focusing on how machines can adapt to human variability. My thesis on adaptive exoskeleton control earned distinction and confirmed my calling: to bridge engineering innovation with healthcare transformation.

Beyond technical expertise, I've cultivated a human-centered design philosophy—every system I develop begins with understanding the person who will use it. This approach has guided my research in rehabilitation robotics, fuzzy logic control, and accessible technology design.

Research & Projects

Hand Exoskeleton for Rehabilitation

A soft robotic exoskeleton for post-stroke hand rehabilitation, featuring adaptive control and real-time biofeedback.

Project Details

This project addresses the critical need for accessible, personalized rehabilitation technology for stroke survivors with hand impairment. The exoskeleton uses soft pneumatic actuators for safe interaction and incorporates EMG and force sensors to detect user intention.

Key innovations include an adaptive fuzzy logic controller that adjusts assistance levels based on user performance and fatigue, and a gamified therapy interface that increases patient engagement by 40% compared to conventional methods.

The system was tested with 15 stroke survivors over 6 weeks, showing an average improvement of 35% in grip strength and 28% in range of motion.

Fuzzy Logic Control of Mobile Robot

An autonomous navigation system for indoor service robots using fuzzy logic for dynamic obstacle avoidance.

Project Details

This research explores fuzzy logic controllers as a robust alternative to traditional navigation algorithms for service robots in dynamic human environments. Unlike conventional approaches, the fuzzy system handles uncertainty and imprecise sensor data more effectively.

The controller uses 27 fuzzy rules to process LiDAR, ultrasonic, and IMU data, generating smooth velocity commands that prioritize human safety and comfort. The system achieves 98.2% obstacle avoidance success in cluttered environments with moving obstacles.

This work was presented at the International Conference on Robotics and Automation and received the Best Student Paper Award in the Intelligent Control category.
